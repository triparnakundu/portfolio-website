From a Small Town in West Bengal to Chennai to Boston to California: Ten Years Away from Home

This move to California comes right after closing a chapter I'm incredibly proud of. I just graduated from Boston University with a 4.0 GPA.

But here's what the GPA doesn't show: the ten years away from home that made it possible, and everything it took to get here.

The Beginning: The First Goodbye

I've been away from home for ten years now. It started when my parents made the difficult decision to send me to boarding school, trading the familiarity of our small town in West Bengal for opportunities in Chennai. I was young, homesick, and uncertain. But my parents understood something I wouldn't fully grasp until much later: sometimes love means letting go so your child can grow.

That first move to Chennai planted a seed. It taught me that discomfort is temporary, but the growth it brings lasts forever. It taught me to adapt, to find family in friendship, to build home wherever I found myself.

When I left Chennai for Boston years later, the distance grew from hundreds of kilometers to thousands of miles. The time zones shifted. The cold became literal and metaphorical. But the foundation my parents built by believing in me enough to send me away remained solid.

The Grind: Building Something from Nothing

When I first arrived in Boston, I started by serving food at BU Dining. It wasn't glamorous, but it was honest work that paid the bills. Coming from a family that sacrificed so much to get me here, I couldn't afford to waste a single opportunity. Every shift, every early morning, every interaction was a chance to learn something about this new world I was navigating.

From there, I worked my way to becoming a Teaching Assistant and Research Assistant. During summers, I served as an Assistant Director at BU Camp, all while taking a full course load during the semester. I published multiple papers, managed several projects simultaneously, and somehow kept all the plates spinning.

Some days meant going from serving breakfast to teaching a class to debugging code for research, all before sitting down to study for my own exams. There were late nights switching between research papers and project deadlines. Moments of exhaustion when I wondered if I could actually balance it all. Times when I missed home so deeply it hurt, thinking about Durga Puja celebrations I was missing or my mother's cooking or just the sound of Bengali spoken naturally around me.

I had to choose between perfect and done, and learned that done (and consistent) often wins.

The Lessons: What Ten Years Taught Me

Those impossible days taught me something valuable: you don't need to have it all figured out. You just need to keep showing up.

I learned to manage my time ruthlessly, prioritize what truly matters, and adapt when plans fell apart (which they often did). Working multiple jobs while maintaining academic excellence taught me resilience, humility, and the value of every opportunity, no matter how small it seems at the start.

One skill I'd been trying to master for a long time finally clicked during my time as an Assistant Director at BU Camp: delegation. I learned that leadership isn't about doing everything yourself. It's about trusting your team, empowering others, and knowing when to step back. That lesson alone was worth the exhaustion.

I also learned that asking for help isn't admitting weakness. It's how you move faster and smarter. And I learned that persistence isn't about never failing; it's about failing, learning, and trying again anyway.

The Gratitude: Standing on Shoulders

None of this would have been possible without the professors who challenged me, the mentors who guided me, the colleagues who collaborated with me, and the friends who reminded me to breathe when everything felt overwhelming. To all of you, thank you.

But most of all, this 4.0 belongs to my parents. The ones who made the impossible choice to send their child away not once, but twice, so he could chase dreams bigger than our small town could contain. Who worked tirelessly so I could focus on my studies. Who stayed up late across time zones just to hear my voice. Who celebrated every small victory and steadied me through every setback.

Ten years away from home, and their faith in me never wavered once.

The Next Chapter: California

Now, as I pack my bags for California, I'm moving again. But this time, it feels different. I'm no longer the homesick kid adjusting to boarding school in Chennai, or the uncertain international student stepping off the plane in Boston. I'm someone who has built a life across continents, who has learned to carry home in his heart rather than search for it on a map.

California represents a new beginning, but it's built on the foundation of every sacrifice my parents made, every lesson boarding school taught me, every early morning shift at BU Dining, every research breakthrough at 2 AM, every moment I chose to keep going when giving up felt easier.

The journey from a small town in West Bengal to Chennai to Boston to California isn't just about the miles traveled. It's about the person I've become along the way. It's about honoring the sacrifices that made this possible. It's about proving that where you start doesn't determine where you finish.

Here's to the next chapter. Ten years away from home, and still going.

To anyone reading this who's far from home, carrying the weight of your family's dreams on your shoulders, wondering if you can make it: you can. Just keep showing up. Your parents believed in you enough to let you go. Now believe in yourself enough to see it through.
